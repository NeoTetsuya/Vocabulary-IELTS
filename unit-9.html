2.2 Introduced Species Reading

Farmers have tried to avoid using pesticides by employing biological methods. In 1925, Australia introduced a moth to control the prickly pear, which spread to 4 million hectares. Reading: Meet the Hedgehog

A Norwich housing development designed for hedgehogs includes paths and holes. Brits love them (Beatrix Potter’s Mrs Tiggy-Winkle). They are good for gardens, eating pests.

B They’ve survived 30 million years. Primitive mammals help geneticists understand evolution and human genomes. Studying them helps understand heart defects.

C 14 species exist in Africa, Asia, Europe. Some hibernate, some handle desert heat. They adapt to urban and remote areas.

D Not strictly solitary; detect neighbors by scent. Scrap over food or aggressive rivalries. Adulthood meetings primarily for mating (hoglets).

E Eat anything: snakes, bird eggs, rodents. Hard insect bodies clean their teeth, preventing gum disease.

F Roll into quill balls. Young spring up to defend. Toxic saliva on quills (from venomous toads) makes quills more painful.

G Thousands die on roads annually. Road kill and intensive farming reduce populations. Clinics and McDonald’s packaging changes help protect them.

H Introduced to Hebrides off Scotland to fight slugs; reduced rare wading birds instead by eating eggs. Relocation preferred over culling.
